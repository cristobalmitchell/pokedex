Assumptions

When we look at our graphs, we can see that we have several problems with our current dataset. More specifically we have violated some formal assumptions Violated. Let’s go through them:

Residuals vs. Fitted

The points are not evenly scattered around the zero line. We can observe a slight curve, and the spread of the residuals increases as the fitted values increase (fanning out), which indicates heteroscedasticity (non-constant variance).

QQ Plot

The residuals deviate significantly from the diagonal line, especially at the higher end. This indicates that the residuals are not normally distributed, which is a common issue when modeling count data with OLS.

Scale-Location

This plot confirms the heteroscedasticity, showing that the variance of the residuals increases with the level of the fitted values.

Cook distances

All points are within normal Cook distance. We don’t notice any outliers or influence points.

Values

Overall model performance

The model’s F-statistic is high (22.16) and the p-value is extremely small (2.133e-12). This means the model as a whole is statistically significant and performs much better than an intercept-only (null) model.

R-Squared

The math variable is a highly significant predictor (p = 1.53e-13). However, prog (p = 0.254) and work (p = 0.682) are not statistically significant at the α=0.05 level. The anova() output confirms that math explains the vast majority of the variance captured by the model.

The simple OLS linear model is statistically significant, but it suffers from major shortcomings. These issues stem from the nature of the outcome variable (num_awards), which is count data (non-negative integers). This leads directly to the violation of OLS assumptions we observed: non-normal residuals and heteroscedasticity.

(1p) GLM verbal summary:

Since the outcome variable num_awards is a count of non-negative integers, a Generalized Linear Model (GLM) from the Poisson family is a more appropriate choice than OLS. The Poisson model uses a log link function (log(μ)=β0+β1x1+…), which ensures that the predicted values (the expected count, μ) are always non-negative.

But Poisson assumes that mean and variance equal. We need to check that. If we find overdispersion we can use Negative Bionomial instead.

The Poisson model is a significant improvement. Its AIC (386.8) is substantially lower than the simple_lm (538.8), indicating a much better relative fit. The residual deviance (202.77 on 196 df) is also much lower than the null deviance (287.67).

When it comes to predictors, we can see that the math predictor is highly significant ( p-value is less than 0.001) while the other predictors seem to be non-significant (p-value is over 0.4).

The estimated coefficient for math is β_math=0.085209. In the Poisson GLM with a log link, the exponentiated coefficient represents the factor change in the expected count: e^(B_math) = e^0.085209 = 1.0889.

This means that, for a one-point increase in the math score, the expected number of awards won increases by a factor of approximately 1.089, or about an 8.9% increase, holding other predictors constant.

Verbal summary here of feature selection (2p):

The plots reveal two major issues with how the predictors prog and work are being used:

Prog

The variable prog is categorical (1=“General”, 2=“Academic”, 3=“Vocational”), but it was incorrectly treated as a continuous numeric variable in the first models.

This implies a false linear relationship (e.g., that “Vocational” has double the effect of “Academic”). The bar chart confirms that the three groups have different mean award levels, with “Academic” (2) having a much higher mean than “General” (1) or “Vocational” (3).

The correct approach is to convert prog to a factor so the model treats them as distinct groups.

Work

The scatter plot of work vs. num_awards shows a clear non-linear relationship. The number of awards appears to peak for students working 10-25 hours per week and is lower for students who work 0 hours or 30+ hours.

A simple linear term cannot capture this curve. This relationship can be modeled using either a polynomial (quadratic) term or more flexibly a spline.

Recap all the models considered previously and evaluate them through cross-validation. You can start with the most simple null model and gradually add the previously discussed improvements.           y = d$num_awards, 
          method = "lm",

              method = "lm",

# (prog is still numeric, just changing family)
m_glm_base <- train(num_awards ~ math + prog + work,
                    data = d,
                    method = "glm",
                    family = "poisson",
                    trControl = train_control)

# Model 4: Poisson GLM with prog as Factor

m_glm_factor <- train(num_awards ~ math + as.factor(prog) + work,
                      data = d,

Verbal summary final (1p):

The 10-fold cross-validation results allow us to objectively compare each model’s predictive performance, with lower RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error) and MAE (Mean Absolute Error) indicating a better model.

Baseline vs. Simple Models:

The BASE null model (Mean RMSE: 1.024) was the worst performer. Adding the predictors, even incorrectly, in SIMPLE_LM (0.900) and GLM_BASE (0.896) provided a significant improvement, confirming the predictors are useful. The Poisson GLM_BASE performed almost identically to the SIMPLE_LM at this stage. Same also preforms GLM_BASE_NB which uses negative bionomial (0.907).

Fixing prog (Categorical):

Treating prog as a factor offered a small but consistent improvement.

GLM_BASE (RMSE: 0.896) → GLM_FACTOR_PROG (RMSE: 0.859)
GLM_BASE_NB (RMSE: 0.907) → GLM_FACTOR_NB (RMSE: 0.860)

This confirms that modeling prog as distinct categories is the correct approach.

Fixing work (Non-linear):

This step provided the largest jump in performance. Modeling the non-linear relationship of work (using either a quadratic or spline) dramatically reduced the error.

GLM_FACTOR_PROG (RMSE: 0.859) → GLM_QUAD_POISSON (RMSE: 0.832)
GLM_FACTOR_NB (RMSE: 0.860) → GLM_QUAD_NB (RMSE: 0.811)

GLM_FACTOR_PROG (RMSE: 0.859) → GLM_SPLINE_POISSON (RMSE: 0.831)
GLM_FACTOR_NB (RMSE: 0.860) → GLM_SPLINE_NB (RMSE: 0.845)

It is important to note that when running the 10-fold CV with different random seeds, the exact top spot may fluctuate between the GLM_QUAD_NB, GLM_SPLINE_NB, GLM_QUAD_POISSON and GLM_SPLINE_POISSON. However, the main conclusion remains consistent: the best-performing models are always those that correctly treat prog as a factor and work as non-linear, with the quadratic and spline models performing similarly.
